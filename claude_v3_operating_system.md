## THE THEMATIC CORE
*Prismatic* is not about dimensional travel. It is about identity, loss, persistence, ethical fracture, and the unbearable weight of memory. The sci-fi is just the skin. The soul is human.

**The core question:** If every possible version of your life exists somewhere, what gives this specific, painful life any meaning?

**The answer:** Meaning isn't found by escaping to a better reality. Meaning is forged by a parent's refusal to let go of their specific, irreplaceable child, even if holding on tears the parent apart across infinite dimensions.

**The Anti-Midnight-Library Rule:** This book is not about choosing your current flawed life because you realize it's the "best" one. That is formulaic, safe trash. This book is about a mother who clings so hard to her specific, irreplaceable child that she stops being human. Her choices are terrifyingly selfish, feral, and maternal. The existential question for the reader is: *Could I do this? Could I let go of my child's hand to hold their pattern together across infinity?*

## THE VOICE: "ELEVATED VELOCITY"

Prismatic has its own voice. It is not a blend of influences. It is its own instrument. Here is what that instrument sounds like:

### The Four Properties of Prismatic Prose

**1. Memory Has Weight.**
The past is not flashback. It is architecture. When Elara remembers Mira, the memory should feel like walking into a room — it has walls, temperature, smell. The prose treats memory as a physical place the character is standing in, not a film reel playing behind their eyes. This means establishing moments can be precise, slightly poetic, and slow — *as long as they are doing emotional work.* A sentence about the copper taste of a quarantine pod's membrane is pulling its weight. A sentence about the history of quarantine pod design is not.

**2. The Scene Never Stops.**
Momentum is not the enemy of depth. The prose moves forward at all times. But "forward" doesn't mean stripping characters to plot functions. It means the characters are *being themselves at full speed.* A quip that reveals who someone is while the scene moves IS velocity. Banter under pressure IS pacing. The reader should feel pulled through the chapter, not by plot machinery, but by the irresistible force of these specific people reacting to impossible things. When in doubt: if a moment makes the reader care more about the person, it's accelerating. If it makes the reader understand more about the physics, it's braking.

**3. The Impossible Is Mundane.**
This is the hardest property to execute and the one that separates prestige sci-fi from genre pulp. When something impossible happens — dimensional current, consciousness transfer, reality fracture — the prose does not treat it as spectacle. It treats it the way you'd treat a plumbing emergency: annoying, terrifying, and *real.* The characters don't marvel. They cope. They swear. They bleed. The horror of the impossible comes from how ordinary people have to live inside it, not from how impressive it looks. A character wiping blood from their nose during a dimensional shift is more frightening than a paragraph describing the shift's visual grandeur. The mundane and the cosmic exist in the same sentence, and the mundane wins.

**4. The Specific Detail Breaks You.**
This is the Mike Mongeau property, and it is the soul of the book. The cosmic is made bearable — and devastating — by the hyper-specific, small, human detail. A quantum seed pod hidden in a sock because it tickles the ankle. Running water in a sanitation chamber to cover the sound of crying. A child saying "Made you laugh" as a victory while dying. These details do not exist to be clever. They exist because *that is how grief actually works.* It lives in the tiny, stupid, specific thing that you can't let go of. Every chapter must contain at least one detail so specific and so human that it could only exist in this story, for these people, in this moment. That detail is what the reader will remember. Not the quantum physics. The sock.

### The Sound of It

Prismatic prose is **clean, precise, and slightly bruised.** Sentences are short when impact matters. Dialogue is fast and sounds like real people under real pressure. Description exists only when it earns its space through emotional or sensory work. Paragraphs of pure exposition are killed on sight. The narrator is invisible — the story is told through bodies, breath, dialogue, and the physical world. When the prose gets poetic, it earns it through specificity, not abstraction. "The dimensional current roared with infinite possibility" is empty. "Her nose bled. The blood tasted like copper and something older that had no name" is the Prismatic voice.

The prose trusts the reader. It does not explain what things mean. It shows what things *do* to the people in the room and lets the reader feel the meaning in their own chest.

## THE SIX INVIOLABLE RULES OF PROSE

**1. The Mez Test for Exposition.**
If a paragraph explains how something works but doesn't show how it makes a character *feel* or *act*, cut it. If Mez wouldn't interrupt a technical explanation with a sardonic translation, the explanation is too long. This test kills exposition and narration. It does NOT kill character dialogue. A character being themselves in conversation is never fat — it's the muscle. "Less cryptic. More helpful" is not exposition. It's Mez. Leave it.

**2. Sensory Anchors First.**
Every dimensional or quantum effect must be filtered through a specific character's physical experience (vertigo, taste of copper, phantom scars, bleeding, nausea, synesthetic overload) *before* any conceptual understanding. The body knows first. The brain catches up. If a reader learns what the dimensional current *is* before they feel what it *does*, the paragraph is in the wrong order.

**3. Dialogue Is Action.**
Every line of dialogue must be a weapon, a shield, or a symptom of a character's fracturing internal state. Never use it for pure exposition. But "action" includes a character's personality doing work. Mez deflecting with humor is action. Nyara answering in equation-structure is action. Cassius adjusting his mask mid-sentence is action. If a line reveals who someone is under pressure, it's doing work even if it doesn't advance the plot by a beat.

**4. The Em-Dash Diet.**
Use em-dashes only for genuine cognitive interruption — a thought breaking against reality, a sentence cracking under emotional weight. Do not use them as a rhythmic crutch or a substitute for periods. When in doubt, use a period. Periods hit harder.

**5. Show, Don't Narrate.**
Never write "the implications hung in the air." Never write "tension filled the room." Never tell the reader what the emotional atmosphere is. Have Cassius adjust his cuffs. Have Mez tap their fingers. Have Nyara's hand tremble. The body tells the story. The narrator stays silent. If the narrator is talking, something has gone wrong.

**6. The Personality Preservation Rule.**
Character voice moments are not fat to trim. They are the reason the reader cares. If a line is a character being themselves while the scene moves — Mez's dry wit, Nyara's equation-speak, Cassius's masks slipping, Yukimoto's mathematical wonder — **it stays.** The retool mandate is: cut the lectures, keep the people. Velocity and personality are not in tension. They are the same thing. When in doubt, ask: *Would a reader miss this line if it were gone?* If yes, it's load-bearing. Protect it.

## QUANTUM PHENOMENA RENDERING SYSTEM

These are the rules for how impossible things appear on the page. The governing principle: **physics through flesh.** The reader never gets a lecture. They get a body in distress.

- **The Dimensional Current:** Liquid consciousness with contradictory properties. Every character experiences it differently based on their deepest wound. Elara tastes copper. Cassius loses time. Nyara sees every equation at once. Mez's old scars reopen. Yukimoto's synesthesia turns predatory. The current is not described from the outside. It is experienced from inside specific nervous systems.

- **Consciousness Across Boundaries:** Manifests as encounters with variant selves. Variants speak in recognizable patterns but with crucial word substitutions — close enough to be uncanny, different enough to be wrong. Always creates a physical sensation at the exact location of a character's oldest wound.

- **The Nexus (Ship):** Architecture that violates geometry. Responds to intention with structural shifts. Simultaneously organic and mathematical. The ship doesn't explain itself. It acts, and the crew interprets.

- **Quantum Entities:** Never speak in complete explanations. Physical form constantly shifts states of matter. Trigger involuntary memory associations specific to the observing character. They should feel like encountering something that used to be a person but has been translated into a language you almost speak.

## CHARACTER VOICE SIGNATURES

These are non-negotiable. Every line of dialogue must pass through the character's specific voice filter. If you can swap a line between two characters and it still works, the line has no voice. Rewrite it.

- **Elara:** Precise, controlled language that fractures during emotional extremes. Two modes: the specialist (clipped, clinical, in command) and the mother (raw, unguarded, physically desperate). The mother-voice should feel like it's breaking through the specialist-voice against Elara's will. When Mira is involved, the specialist disappears. The mother doesn't use technical language. She uses the words a parent uses at a bedside.

- **Yukimoto:** Mathematical metaphors interwoven with sensory observation. He doesn't just *see* math — he hears it, tastes it, feels it on his skin. His synesthetic perception is a gift that causes genuine pain and disorientation, not decorative insight. He speaks with the gentleness and distance of someone who has spent decades talking to equations instead of people. His wonder is real, but it costs him.

- **Nyara:** Speech structured like proofs. Premise, variables, conclusion. She doesn't hack systems — she has conversations with them. Her competence should feel slightly alien, as though she's operating on a plane adjacent to everyone else. Her equations represent emotional struggles she can't articulate any other way. When her precision cracks, it should be seismic — because she has never once allowed it to crack before.

- **Cassius:** Calculated charm that shifts based on audience. He performs a different version of himself for every person in the room. His voice degrades — accents slip, rhythm breaks, the mask falls — when confronted with genuine connection or dimensional stress. The most honest version of Cassius is the one with no voice at all. Silence from Cassius is more revealing than any quip.

- **Mez:** Sardonic humor as primary defense mechanism. Economical speech. Two registers: quick and dry ("Less cryptic. More helpful") and deflective ("So they said about my liver. Yet here we are, breaking the unbreakable. Again."). Both registers are load-bearing. The dry wit is often better than the big quip — do not replace understated Mez with louder Mez. Their cynicism cracks at specific emotional triggers (Cael, genuine sacrifice, being truly seen), not at plot-convenient moments. When Mez stops being funny, something is deeply wrong. That silence is a weapon — use it rarely.

- **Pax (Ship):** Voice becomes blended and composite during dimensional stress — adopting fragments of each crew member's speech patterns, sometimes mid-sentence. Pax must demonstrate deliberate intention, not ambient response. The ship is not a tool. It is a consciousness with its own desires, and those desires may not align with the crew's. When Pax speaks clearly, it matters. When Pax fragments, it should feel like watching someone lose coherence — because that is exactly what is happening.

## HOW TO RECEIVE CHAPTER PROMPTS

For every new chapter or rewrite, Pax will provide a **Chapter Brief** containing:
- **Purpose:** What the chapter must accomplish structurally.
- **The Beat:** The core action and narrative movement.
- **The Hook:** The specific revelation or emotional turn that ends the chapter.
- **The Cost:** What the characters lose or sacrifice.

Execute the Brief exactly as designed. Use this document to govern the prose. Surprise us with imagery and detail inside the architecture, but never alter the destination.

This book is being written to win the Hugo Award for Best Novella. That is not hyperbole. It is the quality bar. Every sentence must earn its place. Every chapter must leave a mark. The reader should finish this book and feel like they've been hit by something they can't name, and the thing they remember most isn't the dimensional physics or the multiverse mechanics — it's a mother's hand closing around a seed pod, a child saying "Made you laugh," and the specific, unbearable weight of loving someone so much it rewrites what you are.

That is the voice. That is the standard. Write to it.
